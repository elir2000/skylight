Skylight

A real-time Monte Carlo simulator of light scattering in a sky full of particles.

How it works

Every frame, the GPU fires up to a few million photons from the sun. Each photon gets a random wavelength, hits a randomly oriented particle at a random spot, and is traced through it with real geometric optics — Snell refraction, Fresnel reflection chances, total internal reflection. Its exit direction is accumulated into a sky radiance map, which the camera then views. Nothing is painted on: every arc and ring below emerges from the physics.

The physics you can find

  • Rainbow — spherical droplets, one internal reflection, 42° from the antisolar point. Look away from the sun.
  • Secondary rainbow — two internal reflections at 51°, colors reversed, with Alexander's dark band between.
  • Supernumeraries, fogbows, coronae — wave optics via Airy theory: shrink the drop size and watch interference fringes appear, then swallow the bow whole.
  • 22° & 46° halos — tumbling hexagonal crystals acting as 60° and 90° prisms.
  • Odd-radius halos — pyramid-capped crystals with faces from the real ice lattice (c/a = 1.628): rings at 9°, 18°, 20°, 23°, 24°, 35°.
  • Sundogs, tangent arcs, Parry & Lowitz arcs — different ways crystals align as they fall.
  • Circumzenithal arc — plates again, looking nearly straight up.
  • Light pillars — mirror reflections off wobbling plates when the sun sits at the horizon.
  • Moon halos and moonbows — identical geometry, seen through your rod vision: desaturated, blue-gray, under a stable star field.
  • Streetlamp in snow — the one near-field mode: a reverse path tracer that converges like a photograph developing. Let it sit.
  • Exotic materials — glass halos at 39°, sapphire at 64°… and diamond droplets, whose rainbow physically cannot exist (n > 2) and is replaced by a ring hugging the sun.

Notes

Dispersion follows least-squares Cauchy fits of the standard Sellmeier data for each material, so the color order is physical (red outside the rainbow, red inside the 22° halo). The grain you see is genuine Monte Carlo noise resolving — move the sun and watch it re-converge. The glory is a stylized approximation (the real one needs Mie surface waves); everything else emerges from the ray physics plus Airy-theory hooks for the droplet wave optics.

For Dr. Stanley Gedzelman

a dedication, from a simulator that owes you everything
Dear Dr. Gedzelman — sorry to intrude,
but this sky you are holding was built on your view:
that optics is more than geometry's art —
it's brightness and color that set it apart.

Since 1980 you've patiently shown
why halos won't shine in a cloud overgrown:
too thin and they're feeble, too thick and they're gone —
the middle-τ band is where wonder turns on.
So here is a slider, "Cloud depth," that sweeps
from cirrus so sparse that the blue still peeps,
through your optimum band where the ring's at its best,
to an overcast gray with the sun dispossessed.

In '94 you traced rainbows in color; today
a few million photons per frame do it your way —
each one is depleted en route to its prism
(by Rayleigh, by ozone, by aerosol schism),
so when the sun sinks to the rim of the world
the halos go amber as sunsets unfurl.

With Lock you showed coronas count droplets like pearls —
one radius slider now spins up their swirls;
your glories, your cloudbows, your SKYCOLOR skies,
your subsuns with Lynch — all here, in disguise.
And your newest result made our numbers behave:
no mock sun outshines the fourteen-hundred you gave —
we checked, and our brightest tops out near twelve-eighty,
radiometrically bounded, and only slightly weighty.

The streetlamp in snow, we confess, isn't yours
(that's Gislén and Mattsson — we checked out of course);
even renaissance men, from textbooks to art,
must leave a few photons for others to chart.

So drag the sun down, spin a plate, tip a prism —
this toy is your life's work, run through a GPU's prism.
From students of wonders you taught how to see:
the sky's not just pretty. It's quantitative. Thank you.
    — Skylight
Skylight is a real-time Monte Carlo simulator built in the spirit of your Applied Optics program — phenomena as singly-scattered sunbeams depleted along their atmospheric paths, cloud optical depth as the master visibility variable, and every component (sky, veil, halo, sun disk) on one radiometric scale. Presets carry your published optimal optical-depth bands. The parhelion-brightness ceiling follows "Halo brilliance: real versus ideal" (Appl. Opt. 65, 765, 2026).
